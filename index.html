Democratic activist Jack Cocchiarella amassed numerous sexual assault allegations at Dartmouth. Now he’s at Columbia and suing for defamation.

A prominent Democrat and Generation Z activist interested in pursuing a career in politics, Jack Cocchiarella began attending Dartmouth College in fall 2021. Within his first two months at the college, however, several of his peers accused him of serial rape and sexual assault on various social media platforms.

To end the alleged “smear campaign,” Cocchiarella, CC ’25, transferred to Columbia College this fall and recently filed a defamation lawsuit against a Dartmouth student he claims is responsible for the social media posts. But the allegations continue to follow him, both across the colleges’ campuses and online.

With nearly 300,000 followers on Twitter, over 17,000 on Instagram, and nearly 20,000 on TikTok, Cocchiarella is a popular political news analyst from Winter Park, Florida. He has built a name for himself—one the suit said he’s looking to “finally clear.”

“He’s something of a celebrity,” Damien Fisher, a journalist at the NHJournal who reported on the allegations against Cocchiarella in August, told Spectator. “He certainly leveraged his status as an activist, as this sort of Gen Z star, and he’s leveraging it to appear on TV, to podcast, and to get contracts with political campaigns as a consultant.”

A lawsuit filed in September in the District Court of New Hampshire by Cocchiarella and Politically Correct Strategies LLC, his Florida-based limited liability company, is seeking damages of over $75,000 for defamation, intentional infliction of emotional distress, and tortious interference with economic relationships from Nate Kim, a former peer and current Dartmouth sophomore.

When reached for comment, Kim directed Spectator to his attorney Benjamin King, a specialist in personal injury law and a partner at the New Hampshire firm Douglas, Leonard & Garvey. In an interview with Spectator, King said, “It’s befuddling why the plaintiff has targeted Mr. Kim as a defendant.”

While Cocchiarella’s attorneys wholly deny the accusations in legal filings and attribute them to his conservative opponents, multiple sources familiar with the Dartmouth students who accused Cocchiarella told Spectator the allegations have no political motivations. Cocchiarella and his attorneys declined to comment for this article.

As Cocchiarella attempts to integrate into the Columbia community amid media coverage of his allegations and a public lawsuit, sources cited in the suit worry about his presence on the University’s campus, while his lawyers remain concerned about whether his reputation can be salvaged.

College Republicans, allegations, and ‘social ostracization’ at Dartmouth

On Oct. 24, the Dartmouth College Republicans hosted an event titled “The Future of the Republican Party” for roughly 150 students in the school’s Filene Auditorium. Among other Republican lawmakers, the panel featured Rep. Madison Cawthorn, a conservative from North Carolina who was involved in the Jan. 6 insurrection and is considered by many to be one of the “most far-right” members of Congress.

During the event, about 50 students protested outside the auditorium, holding signs and chanting slogans including “Fascists, go home.” Multiple students in attendance walked out of the event or shouted at the panelists, The Dartmouth reported.

One of the panelists’ challengers included Cocchiarella, who confronted Cawthorn regarding his false claims of fraud in the election of President Joe Biden.

“So when will you stop lying to your constituents, admit that Donald Trump lost, and end this senseless attack on our democracy?” Cocchiarella asked during the event.

His question immediately evolved into a heated debate between Cocchiarella, Cawthorn, and other participants. Caught on video, the interaction went viral on social media and circulated among several national news outlets including CNN and NBC. At the time of this article’s publication, it had over 2 million views and 61,000 likes on Twitter.

Cocchiarella’s attorneys claim this College Republicans event catalyzed the “false and disparaging” social media posts accusing him of rape and sexual assault that arose soon after, according to the defamation lawsuit he filed in September.

The first allegations surfaced anonymously on Reddit, the college social app Fizz, and Librex, a now-deleted discussion forum app for Ivy League students. A screenshot obtained by Spectator of a post from Librex—which shut down in February, deleting the data of almost 2,000 users at Dartmouth—details the account of an alleged survivor of Cocchiarella, who wrote they were raped by him in their dorm room.

In the Librex post, the anonymous user alleged that they knew one other student Cocchiarella had raped and “several others” he had sexually assaulted “in ways that weren’t sex.” The Librex user claims they reported Cocchiarella to Dartmouth’s Title IX office and were then told by the office that the department was “incredibly familiar” with him. The post also said that when the user reported Cocchiarella to Title IX, another student had already filed a no-contact order against him.

“Hes known to grope girls in frat basements and even public,” the Librex user wrote.

A Reddit post from December 2021 with 1,128 upvotes referenced the Librex post and said Cocchiarella has been accused of “raping 8 of his classmates” in his first term at Dartmouth. Another post from the same anonymous user, @throwaway48281418581, told students to “[b]eware” and refers to Cocchiarella as a campus “serial rapist.”

“He (allegedly) uses his Twitter notoriety and left-wing credentials to position himself as an ally,” @throwaway48281418581 wrote. “Then, once people let their guards down, he rapes them.”

Before the allegations of sexual assault arose against Cocchiarella, Christina and Nicholas—two current Dartmouth sophomores named using pseudonyms, who spoke to Spectator on the condition of anonymity, citing fear of legal retaliation—described him as “well-known” and “friends with a lot of people” at Dartmouth. Christina said Cocchiarella was “one of those people on campus that everyone kind of knows their name.”

Neither Christina nor Nicholas had personal relationships with Cocchiarella beyond mutual friends, but Nicholas said he had stopped by Cocchiarella’s room with other students on one occasion.

“I actually met him on the first day that I got here. ... There wasn’t anything unusual,” Nicholas said. “As the year went on and more and more allegations came out, it was easily the most difficult thing not to know about because everyone had heard everything.”

Following the publicization of his allegations, Cocchiarella was blacklisted from all fraternities on campus and several student groups, Christina and Nicholas said. One organization included Dartmouth College Democrats, which in a July 28 tweet said it asked Cocchiarella to “leave the club in late October 2021, following campus-wide allegations of sexual assault.”

“Dartmouth is such a small school,” Christina said. “If your name gets tainted in any way, everyone is going to know.”

The tweet from Dartmouth College Democrats describing Cocchiarella’s removal from the group has since been deleted, and the group wrote in August that it does not “condone” any use of the deleted tweet. The club did not respond to repeated requests for comment at the time of publication.

Because most students were aware of the allegations, Cocchiarella often ate alone at the dining halls and was “never with anyone” on campus, Nicholas said. “He was completely ostracized,” he said.

“Nobody could willingly hang out with him just because it was so criminal by association. It was very damning to be caught with him,” Nicholas said. “You could not really get away with hanging out with him in any capacity.”

Both Christina and Nicholas said they know students who previously told them Cocchiarella made them uncomfortable. Fisher said in an interview with Spectator that he was in contact with a woman who had experienced “unwanted touching” by Cocchiarella. Fisher said he obtained documents from Dartmouth’s Title IX office signed by Cocchiarella detailing the woman’s allegations and potential disciplinary action.

Dartmouth’s spokespeople and Title IX office did not respond to repeated requests for comment at the time of publication. Although Fisher claimed to have evidence of Title IX action taken against Cocchiarella at the university level, he said he filed a right-to-know request with the Hanover Police Department and found that there were no reports of sexual assault made to the city’s police involving Cocchiarella.

In a recent statement he tweeted in August, Cocchiarella denied the “false attacks” against him and said that it was only after he confronted Cawthorn at the Dartmouth College Republicans event that people “spread lies intended to hurt [his] reputation” and “end [his] work in politics” on various social media platforms.

“I state unequivocally that I have never engaged in or been investigated for any such conduct,” Cocchiarella wrote. “I am deeply troubled by the weaponization of something as serious as sexual assault. There is no truth to any of the anonymous attacks against me, and I am working closely with the authorities to uncover the source of these lies.”

Transferring to Columbia and Dartmouth Title IX

After facing what the lawsuit called a “toxic environment” and social ostracization at Dartmouth, Cocchiarella applied to transfer to Columbia last spring and received his decision by June 1. According to the suit, one of Kim’s alleged accounts tweeted on Aug. 11 that he sent at least one email to Columbia warning the administration of the sexual assault allegations against Cocchiarella, although the date of that email was not specified.

Columbia takes into account the disciplinary records of potential transfer applicants, a University spokesperson told Spectator. The spokesperson wrote that the University adheres to admission policies meant to create a student body that positively contributes to the campus community, but provided no specific comment about Cocchiarella.

According to Cocchiarella’s attorneys, Dartmouth issued a letter on July 25 stating that Cocchiarella was “in good academic standing” and had “no disciplinary record,” but did not specify to whom the letter was sent.

At Dartmouth, the Title IX office offers one of two paths of resolution based on a student’s report: formal or informal. The formal resolution process triggers an investigation, resulting in an official report and culminating in a live hearing with both parties present. If the fault is attributed to the accused, the formal resolution process results in disciplinary action and sanctions.

The informal resolution process, however, does not result in any disciplinary action. It can still result in “interim remedial measures” or “community remedies,” according to a document from the Dartmouth Title IX office. These informal measures may include a separation order or no-contact order, like that mentioned in the Librex post.

Spectator could not confirm whether or not informal, non-disciplinary processes would have been reflected in Cocchiarella’s transfer application to Columbia. Christina said she knows that students intended to go to Dartmouth Title IX with the allegations against Cocchiarella, but could not say with certainty what action was taken, if any.

Now, students at Columbia have raised concerns about Cocchiarella’s past and the sexual assault allegations he amassed at Dartmouth, according to the lawsuit. His peers at the University have told him that “they cannot associate with Jack until his name is cleared,” the filings state.

Cocchiarella v. Kim and its ties to politics

The September lawsuit claims Cocchiarella has been “the target of a calculated smear campaign by Kim through the propagation of false and fabricated information” over various online platforms such as Twitter. It states that Kim’s posts on social media intended to “silence” Cocchiarella and “permanently damage his private and public reputation,”“presumably in retaliation” against his actions at the Dartmouth College Republicans event.

Spectator could not find any official affiliation between Kim and the Dartmouth College Republicans other than the speculations presented in the lawsuit.

Attorneys Susan Stone, a Title IX specialist from Ohio-based Kohrman Jackson & Krantz LLP who has experience in defending the “wrongly or unlawfully accused,” and Kevin Collimore, a partner at New Hampshire-based Cullen Collimore Shirley PLLC, are representing Cocchiarella in his civil case against Kim. Stone and Collimore declined to comment.

According to the suit, Kim’s conduct has provoked others to make death threats against Cocchiarella, with the most recent being from Sept. 15. “To this day, Jack lives in fear for his life and safety as a result of the False Statements and Lies spread by Kim,” the filing reads.

Twenty-six pages of the lawsuit feature screenshots of posts Kim has allegedly made since November 2021. Based upon “information and belief,” the lawsuit alleges that Kim operates three public Twitter accounts—@NKim57, @stoprapists101, and @aplopl3x—which he has used to tweet about the accusations of sexual assault against Cocchiarella.

A series of tweets from @stoprapists101 in the lawsuit explicitly call Cocchiarella a rapist and reference the “multiple rape allegations against him that have been corroborated by multiple witnesses.” The account often replied to tweets made by Cocchiarella under his handle @JDCocchiarella, attaching the initial Librex post and demanding him to “stop raping my classmates.”

“It appears you don’t know what consent is, when you raped 6 girls on our campus in one term and then called the allegations false and blamed it on the Dartmouth Republicans,” @stoprapists101 wrote in a March tweet. “Stop pretending like you care about women’s rights.”

Tweets from both @stoprapists101 and @aplopl3x deny political motives for the allegations against Cocchiarella, and a thread from @aplopl3x claims the Dartmouth College Republicans would have had to “convince 8 (mostly liberal) women” to falsely accuse Cocchiarella.

King, Kim’s attorney, said in an interview with Spectator that several news outlets such as the NHJournal also publicized Cocchiarella’s sexual assault allegations and that Kim was “not the source for any of these media articles.”

“All Mr. Kim has done is repeat allegations made by others on social media, and these allegations are now the subject of media articles,” King said. “These media articles … would seem to be a more obvious cause of reputational harm to Mr. Cocchiarella than anything repeated by Mr. Kim on Twitter.”

In a lawsuit like Cocchiarella v. Kim, the burden of proof of defamation differs depending on whether the plaintiff is considered a public figure or not, King explained. Under New Hampshire law, there is a liability for statements made against a non-public figure if they were made “in the absence of reasonable care.” For public figures, however, there is liability only if the plaintiff can prove “actual malice,” which is a “far higher hurdle,” King said.

The question of Cocchiarella’s status as a public figure refers to his prolific social media presence and following as a self-described Gen Z activist and Democratic digital strategist. It has not been decided whether Cocchiarella will be considered a public figure or not in the lawsuit, and King said he expects it to be an issue that will be addressed later on in the case.

Cocchiarella has also worked as a consultant on the campaigns of prominent Democrats, including gubernatorial candidate Charlie Crist running against Gov. Ron DeSantis in Florida and Georgia’s 14th District Congressional candidate Marcus Flowers, the opponent of Rep. Marjorie Taylor Greene. According to data from the Florida Department of State’s Divison of Elections and the Federal Election Commission, both Crist and Flowers disbursed money to Cocchiarella’s Politically Correct Strategies LLC in the past year, paying $2,250 in July and $41,000 total from February to June, respectively—after the sexual assault allegations arose.

A primary concern of the lawsuit deals with the “economic injuries,” loss of “lucrative business opportunities,” and harm to Cocchiarella’s political “career aspirations” that he and his LLC have experienced as a result of Kim allegedly spreading his sexual assault allegations online. On at least one occasion, Dartmouth Title IX emailed a prospective business associate of Cocchiarella in August confirming he had never been the subject of a Title IX investigation, the lawsuit said.

Despite Kim’s alleged Twitter accounts negating the allegations’ connections to Cocchiarella’s political career and Democratic platform, one of the tweets from @stoprapists101 included in the lawsuit appeared to defend Kenosha shooter Kyle Rittenhouse in response to Cocchiarella’s tweet about the Wisconsin court’s verdict.

“Since when have you guys cared about borders when you’re totally fine letting unvaccinated savages come into the country along the southern border,” @stoprapists101 wrote. “Kyle worked and had family in Kenosha.”

@stoprapists101’s tweet is part of a much larger trend of the conservative-dominated online Twitter discourse surrounding Cocchiarella’s sexual assault allegations. Under the #JackCocchiarella hashtag, several of the top tweets come from accounts espousing right-wing rhetoric, with one account labeling itself as “Not Safe For Liberals” and another as a MAGA Trump supporter. An article about allegations against Cocchiarella by the Washington Free Beacon, a conservative journalism website, has been widely shared within the hashtag.

Although much of the public discussion of Cocchiarella’s sexual assault allegations has come from right-wing Twitter users, Nicholas, Christina, and Fisher told Spectator that the students who they know had uncomfortable encounters with Cocchiarella had no political motivations. Christina said, “politics had nothing to do with it.”

“You can’t discount any possibility, right, when it comes to politics, my goodness,” Fisher said. “But I don’t have any direct evidence that that’s the reason why this has come out. I don’t have anyone … who I’ve seen who is actually benefiting from this in any way.”

The Dartmouth student Nicholas knows who was allegedly made uncomfortable by Cocchiarella is a “left-leaning student” and would not have wanted to “further the agenda” of conservatives on campus, he said. The student declined an interview with Spectator.

“If it was something that was a product of some sort of drive to get rid of him, I really cannot believe that,” Nicholas said.

The legacy of the Trump administration’s Betsy DeVos era

Recent Title IX rules established under former Secretary of Education Betsy DeVos narrow the definition of sexual harassment and expand the rights of the accused by allowing students to cross-examine accusers in live hearings.

Women’s rights groups have expressed that the new Title IX policy, which was not permitted under the Obama administration, may “discourage sexual assault victims from coming forward,” the Washington Post reported.

By mandate, Dartmouth adopted cross-examinations in its Title IX investigation process in July 2020. Diana Whitney—a Dartmouth alumna and co-founder of the advocacy group Dartmouth Community against Gender Harassment & Sexual Violence—said the prospect of mandatory live cross-examinations has influenced the decisions of past students whom she has worked with who are considering filing formal Title IX complaints.

“I know this survivor I was talking with was really afraid that there was going to be basically a cross-examination by the peers of the alleged perpetrator,” Whitney said about a student unrelated to Cocchiarella’s allegations. “It has been in the conversation and this sort of level of fear around it is definitely great.”

Four tweets from @aplopl3x, one of the Twitter accounts mentioned in the lawsuit, raise concerns about the fact that Cocchiarella’s victims would “have to face” him in cross-examination for a Title IX investigation to continue. @aplopl3x claims they got this information from anonymous survivors of Cocchiarella on Librex.

Whitney also said that “perpetrators have been emboldened by Trump era and DeVos era changes.”

Dartmouth has a long and public history of mishandling cases of survivors of sexual assault on its campus. In 2019, nine women won a settlement for $14.4 million after Dartmouth repeatedly overlooked the sexual abuse of several graduate students by three professors for about 17 years. A letter from nearly 800 Dartmouth alumni and students supporting the plaintiffs in that class action case references “an institutional culture that minimizes and disregards sexual violence and gender harassment.”

An anonymous sexual misconduct survey administered to Dartmouth students in 2022 revealed 13 percent of 1,251 respondents reported experiencing “nonconsensual penetration or sexual touching either through physical force, incapacitation, or coercion.” That percentage is higher than a previous survey from 2021 but lower than surveys from 2015 and 2017, and it is on par with the national average of 13 percent.

[Datawrapper #1 of the survey]

Of the students who reported experiencing sexual violence at Dartmouth in the 2022 survey, 67 percent said they did not tell anyone about the incident before responding to the survey. Fourteen percent of those who said they didn’t report said it was because they thought nothing would be done.

[Datawrapper #2 of the survey]

The Biden administration has proposed new Title IX rules that, among other measures, will eliminate live cross-examinations and protect students, employees, and others who exercise their Title IX rights from retaliation. The new rules opened for public comment in June, but it is unclear when they will go into effect.

“As we celebrate the 50th Anniversary of this landmark law, our proposed changes will allow us to continue that progress and ensure all our nation's students – no matter where they live, who they are, or whom they love – can learn, grow, and thrive in school,” U.S. Secretary of Education Miguel Cardona wrote in a press release.

The Oct. 6 hearing and next steps in Cocchiarella v. Kim

In a hearing that took place over Zoom last Thursday, the two parties discussed two motions: a preliminary injunction, asking Kim to stop tweeting about Cocchiarella and issue a retraction of his claims, and an expedited discovery, requesting to speed up the process of obtaining information from Kim.

The court did not rule on the preliminary injunction but ruled affirmatively for expedited discovery.

The motion for an injunction, which lies at the heart of the case, demands two types of relief—prohibitory and mandatory. The prohibitory injunction would require Kim to never post allegations against Cocchiarella in the future, while the mandatory injunction would require Kim to issue a retraction of all statements he has allegedly made in the past.

Throughout the oral arguments, Stone expressed a desire for the proceedings to happen quickly so that Cocchiarella’s reputation could be restored. King argued that the merits of the case were not strong enough to even proceed with discovery, saying that the monetary damages could not directly be linked to Kim’s comments.

In the discovery process to come, Kim will be subject to deposition and must provide usernames to all his Reddit, Fizz, Librex, and Twitter handles and any posts he has made under those accounts. Kim will also be asked to provide information on all conversations—not limited to online contact—with alleged survivors of Cocchiarella.

Lawsuits like this can take up to a year to resolve, King said, with the possibility of a jury trial. The court ordered Kim to produce the documents requested in the discovery by Nov. 7.

At Dartmouth, news of the sexual assault allegations against Cocchiarella and the lawsuit continues to circulate, even among first-year students, Christina said. Screenshots obtained by Spectator of Dartmouth’s Fizz page reveal students have made posts about Cocchiarella as recently as Tuesday.

“When the topic of sexual assault awareness comes up, I think his name is always kind of in the mix,” Christina said.
